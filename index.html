Moderator
Sarah Brennan
Senior Correspondent, World Affairs Review
Twenty-year veteran journalist who covered refugee crises from Rwanda to the Mediterranean. Author of Borders and Bodies: A Reporter's Chronicle. Winner of the International Press Freedom Award.
The Refugee
Linh Nguyen
Vietnamese Refugee, 1979 Boat Person — now San Jose, California
Fled Vietnam at age seven aboard an overcrowded fishing vessel with her mother and three siblings. Spent four months in a UNHCR camp in Malaysia. Lost her younger sister at sea. Earned a doctorate in public policy and now advocates for refugee rights at the UN.
Host Country Official
Datuk Haris Yusof
Commissioner (ret.), Malaysian Ministry of Home Affairs
Served on the front lines of Malaysia's response to the 1979 boat people crisis, coordinating with UNHCR on refugee camp operations in Pulau Bidong. Now a visiting fellow at the University of Malaya, specializing in Southeast Asian migration policy.
Sending Country Official
Ambassador Trần Quang Minh
Former Deputy Foreign Minister, Socialist Republic of Vietnam
Veteran of the People's Army, served in diplomatic posts in Moscow and Geneva. A defender of the revolutionary government's policies in the post-war period, now retired but remains a spokesperson for the official Vietnamese historical narrative.
— Transcript of Panel Discussion —
Brennan
Sarah Brennan — Moderator Welcome. Today we sit at the intersection of history and conscience. April 30, 1975 — the fall of Saigon. Over the next two decades, an estimated two million Vietnamese would flee their homeland. Between 200,000 and 250,000 of them died at sea. I want to begin with you, Linh. You were seven years old when you boarded a fishing boat in the South China Sea. Take us back.
Linh
Linh Nguyen — Refugee I remember my mother wrapping me in a life jacket made of old foam and sewn-together fabric. We paid gold — everything my family had left after the government seized our home — to board a fishing boat built to carry thirty people. There were more than two hundred of us. The smell, the heat, the crying children. When Thai pirates came on the second night, they took our remaining gold, our food, our dignity. They threw two men overboard when they resisted. We were at sea for eleven days before a Malaysian coast guard vessel found us. I still see the colour of that water in my dreams.
Brennan
Sarah Brennan Datuk Yusof — by 1979, Malaysia had received over 100,000 Vietnamese boat people UNHCR. What was the mood inside the Malaysian government?
Yusof
Datuk Haris Yusof — Host Country Official I will be honest with you — as I was not always permitted to be at the time. Malaysia is a country with a delicate ethnic balance: Malay Muslims, Chinese Malaysians, Indian Malaysians. The refugees were arriving overwhelmingly in our most conservative Muslim coastal areas, and many were of Chinese cultural background. That created genuine domestic political tensions. Our government adopted the "port of first asylum" policy in July 1979, but it was always conditional. We could provide temporary refuge; we could not absorb hundreds of thousands permanently. We needed the world to honour its share of the burden — and for a period, remarkably, it did. ADST.org
Brennan
Sarah Brennan Ambassador Minh — historians are unequivocal. The re-education camps held over one million people Wikipedia/Indochina Refugee Crisis. Businesses were seized. Ethnic Chinese were purged. People were sent to "New Economic Zones" as forced labour. How do you account for this mass flight of your own citizens?
Minh
Amb. Trần Quang Minh — Vietnamese Government Every revolution demands restructuring. South Vietnam had been a client state serving American military interests for thirty years. The "re-education" — yes, that was the term used — was an effort at national reconciliation, not extermination. Many of those who fled were not innocent civilians. They were administrators of a foreign-backed regime, intelligence operatives, military officers who had waged war against their own people. Vietnam is today a unified nation and a rising economy. History, I would argue, has shown the revolution was necessary. The United States killed over three million Vietnamese during the war History.com. We do not hear Washington apologising for that.
Linh
Linh Nguyen Ambassador — my father was a schoolteacher. Not a general. Not a spy. A primary school teacher who had attended, once, an American-funded professional development seminar in 1968. For that, he spent three years in a camp near the Cambodian border. When they released him, he was a different man. His hands shook. He could not sleep. That is why we built a raft and ran. So please do not give me the theory of the revolution. Give me back my father. Give me back my sister who drowned.
— a silence fell over the room —
Yusof
Datuk Haris Yusof I want to add something to what Linh said. The boats that came to our beaches were not carrying politicians. They were carrying fishermen, teachers, shopkeepers, young mothers. In November 1978, a freighter called the Hai Hong arrived at Port Klang with 2,500 Vietnamese aboard. My government demanded it turn back to sea. I was the officer who had to transmit that order. I have not forgotten those faces at the railing. The UNHCR intervened and they were eventually processed Vietnamese Museum / UNHCR history. But that moment taught me: international law is only as strong as political will.
Brennan
Sarah Brennan Ambassador — 54 percent of Americans in May 1975 opposed admitting Vietnamese refugees Gallup / Duke Migration Memorials. The world was not rushing to help. What responsibility does Vietnam's government bear for the deaths at sea?
Minh
Amb. Trần Quang Minh The people who died at sea made choices. We did not force them onto those boats. The government offered legal pathways — for some. The Orderly Departure Programme of 1979 was eventually an international framework that allowed emigration through official channels. If people chose pirate boats and open water, that was a decision born of fear — some of it manufactured by foreign-funded propaganda.
Linh
Linh Nguyen My mother applied for the Orderly Departure Programme. We were denied. Twice. The "legal pathway" was available to those with connections and bribes. We had neither. The sea was not our first choice, Ambassador. It was our only one.
Brennan
Sarah Brennan Datuk Yusof — we end with this. The world today has 123.2 million forcibly displaced people as of 2024 UNHCR Global Trends 2024. Turkey alone hosts 2.9 million Syrians. What lesson from your experience in 1979 would you deliver to any government facing this today?
Yusof
Datuk Haris Yusof No nation can carry this alone. That is the single lesson. In 1979, the United States and others made a political commitment: we will take these people off your hands if you give them safe harbour while we process them. That compact worked. Within eighteen months, 450,000 Indochinese refugees were resettled The Globalist. Today, that compact is broken. Wealthy nations build walls and pass the cost to Jordan, Uganda, Bangladesh, Turkey. And then they are surprised when those countries push boats back to sea. We did it too. I am not proud of it. But I understand it.
Linh
Linh Nguyen — Closing Statement I want people to remember one thing. Vietnamese-Americans today number 1.5 million EBSCO Research Starters. We built businesses, raised children, won scholarships, paid taxes. We contribute. Every generation of refugees the world has bet on has returned that bet with interest. The question is never whether refugees can make it. The question is whether we will let them try.

Expert — Refugee Perspective
Prof. Amara Nwosu
Professor of Refugee Law, University of Geneva
Nigerian-born legal scholar and author of The Right to Flee. Served as a legal advisor to UNHCR for twelve years across three continents. Specialises in statelessness, non-refoulement, and the gap between refugee law as written and as practised.
Expert — Host Country Perspective
Dr. Patrick Holloway
Senior Migration Policy Analyst, Chatham House
Former UNHCR field officer in Malaysia and Thailand during the 1980s, now a policy advisor to three European governments on refugee integration. Author of Burden Sharing: The Broken Compact. Known for rigorous, politically uncomfortable analysis.
Expert — Comparative / Historical
Dr. Yuki Tanaka
Professor of Contemporary History, University of Oxford
Japanese-Australian historian whose research spans the Cold War refugee crises from Hungary 1956 to Vietnam 1975 to Afghanistan 2021. Argues that every U.S. military withdrawal in the 20th and 21st centuries has generated a structurally identical refugee crisis.
— Transcript of Expert Panel —
Brennan
Sarah Brennan For fifty years, the Vietnamese boat people crisis has been held up as either the great example of international solidarity — or the great example of international failure. Professor Nwosu, which is it?
Nwosu
Prof. Amara Nwosu — Refugee Rights It is both, at different moments. The 1979 Geneva Conference was a genuine breakthrough — the first time the international community agreed to treat Indochinese boat arrivals as prima facie refugees, entitled to protection rather than prosecution. That principle produced the Orderly Departure Programme and the Comprehensive Plan of Action of 1989, which ultimately resettled over 2.5 million Indochinese across North America, Europe, and Australia Wikipedia / UNHCR. That is solidarity. But the decade before Geneva? Malaysia pushing boats back to sea. Thailand towing vessels into international waters. Singapore refusing to allow disembarkation unless resettlement was guaranteed within ninety days Vietnamese Museum. People drowned because of those policies. That is failure. The crisis was both, and the lesson is that it tips toward solidarity only under enormous, sustained international political pressure.
Holloway
Dr. Patrick Holloway — Host Country Policy I want to complicate Amara's framing slightly — not to defend the pushbacks, which were indefensible, but to understand the structural logic. By mid-1979, for every one refugee resettled from Southeast Asia, three more were arriving Vietnamese Museum. Host governments were not dealing with a manageable crisis — they were staring at an exponentially growing one. Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia: none had signed the 1951 Refugee Convention. They had no legal obligation under international law. What eventually worked was not moral appeals — it was a deal. Wealthy nations said: "Give us time to process them; we'll take them off your plate." That is burden-sharing, and it is a fundamentally different argument from "it's the right thing to do." Governments respond to interests as much as ideals.
Tanaka
Dr. Yuki Tanaka — Comparative History What strikes me, historically, is the almost eerie structural similarity between 1975 and 2021. In both Vietnam and Afghanistan, the United States withdrew militarily after a failed ideological project. In both cases, a new ideological regime — communist in one, theocratic in the other — consolidated power rapidly. In both cases, those associated with the previous American-backed order faced persecution: re-education camps in Vietnam, Taliban tribunals in Afghanistan. And in both cases, a mass exodus followed — roughly two million over twenty years for Vietnam; a new Afghan wave still unfolding Journal of Political Science and International Relationship, 2024. The same debate recycled: "Are they genuine refugees or economic migrants?" The same answer, visible in retrospect: they were both, and the distinction was always a political weapon, not a legal one.
Brennan
Sarah Brennan Dr. Tanaka — let us stay on that comparison. The language of "genuine refugee vs. economic migrant" that was used against Vietnamese boat people in the 1970s — we hear it today about Venezuelans, Afghans, Syrians. Is history simply repeating?
Tanaka
Dr. Yuki Tanaka Not simply repeating — rhyming. The specifics differ but the political grammar is identical. In May 1975, fifty-four percent of Americans opposed admitting Vietnamese refugees Gallup cited in Duke Migration Memorials. A decade later, those same Vietnamese-Americans were running restaurants, engineering firms, and university departments that transformed cities like San Jose, Houston, and New Orleans. By the year 2000, 1.5 million Vietnamese-Americans were woven into the fabric of American economic and civic life EBSCO Research Starters. The historical verdict on the "genuine refugee" question is in: they were genuine, they contributed enormously, and the fear was largely unfounded. Every generation of policy makers seems to need to relearn this lesson from scratch.
Nwosu
Prof. Amara Nwosu The rhetoric really is almost word-for-word. And I want to make the legal point clearly: under the 1951 Refugee Convention, the distinction between "political refugee" and "economic migrant" is supposed to be about the nature of the persecution — not the person's economic condition. A Vietnamese farmer who fled because his land was seized and he faced forced relocation to a New Economic Zone Wikipedia / Indochina Refugee Crisis — that is persecution. The fact that he was also poor does not make him an economic migrant. The conflation of the two has always been a policy tool, not a legal one. And today, with 42.5 million refugees globally as of mid-2025 UNHCR Mid-Year Trends 2025, the stakes of that conflation have never been higher.
Brennan
Sarah Brennan Dr. Holloway — you've been the sceptic on this panel. Make the hard case for the host country. What are the genuine limits of generosity?
Holloway
Dr. Patrick Holloway The hard case is this: integration is a public policy challenge, not just a moral gesture. The second wave of Vietnamese boat people — arriving in the late 1970s — were, as a group, less educated, less English-proficient, and arrived during an American economic recession History.com. Integration was slower. There were real incidents of community tension, economic competition, and anti-Vietnamese violence. It was eventually managed — but it required investment: language programmes, employment support, community infrastructure. Generosity without investment produces backlash. Today, seventy-one percent of the world's refugees are hosted by low and middle income countries UNHCR Global Trends. Turkey hosts 2.9 million Syrians. Uganda hosts 1.9 million Africans. These are not wealthy nations. The moral demand that they remain open borders while rich nations fund nothing and take no one is not a refugee policy — it is an abdication.
Nwosu
Prof. Amara Nwosu — What Must Be Revived Which brings me to the one Vietnamese-era policy I would most urgently revive: the Orderly Departure Programme. Instead of forcing people onto the Andaman Sea or the Mediterranean — instead of turning those waters into mass graves — the world needs legal, safe pathways for people to apply for protection from within or near their home countries. The 1979 programme eventually saved tens of thousands of lives Wikipedia / Boat People. Today, in-country processing for Syria, Sudan, Myanmar — it is technically feasible. It is politically abandoned. First-half 2025 resettlements are already nearly three times lower than the same period in 2024 UNHCR Mid-Year 2025. We are not moving forward. We are in reverse.
Tanaka
Dr. Yuki Tanaka — Closing Statement I want to end on something that matters: the former enemy. Ho Chi Minh City — Saigon — is today one of Southeast Asia's fastest-growing economies. Vietnam is among the United States' major trading partners. The country that generated one of the largest refugee crises of the twentieth century now participates in international forums on refugee protection. Former enemies become partners. Former refugees — the ones who survived, the ones we let in — built new worlds in places that feared them. The arc of history, when we choose to bend it, tends to surprise us with how well it turns out. The question for 2026 is whether we will remember that lesson or repeat the same costly amnesia.

Did America Do the Right Thing?
The Debate Over Vietnamese Refugees

It is 1979. A journalist hosts a panel bringing together four very different Americans — a refugee herself, a U.S. government official, a Congressman who opposed the programme, and a Vietnamese communist diplomat. They debate whether America made the right call letting in thousands of strangers from a war America helped start — and what it means for the world today.

— Meet the Panellists —
Moderator / Journalist
Margaret Chen
Anchor, National Public Broadcasting — 1979
A seasoned journalist born to Chinese-American parents, Margaret has covered refugee stories from Cuba to Indochina. She is known for asking tough questions of both politicians and survivors.
The Refugee
Linh Thi Nguyen
Vietnamese Refugee · arrived Fort Chaffee, Arkansas, 1975
Linh fled Saigon at age 9 during Operation Frequent Wind — the chaotic US helicopter evacuation from the embassy roof. She and her mother were among 130,000 Vietnamese given "parolee" status by President Ford. Now 13, she speaks through a translator.
U.S. Government Official
Julia Taft
Director, Inter-Agency Task Force on Indochinese Refugee Resettlement (real historical figure)
President Ford appointed Julia Taft to lead America's refugee response. She coordinated the four military bases — Camp Pendleton, Fort Chaffee, Fort Indiantown Gap, and Eglin Air Force Base — that processed 130,000 refugees in 1975. She believed America had a moral duty.
U.S. Congressman — Opposing View
Rep. John Marsh (fictional composite)
U.S. Congressman — Opponent of Refugee Programme, 1975
Based on real objections raised in Congress in 1975. Represents constituents concerned about unemployment (nearly 9%), welfare costs, and cultural change. He is not a villain — he genuinely believes he is protecting American workers.
Vietnamese Government Diplomat
Nguyễn Văn Long
Vietnamese Government Representative — UN, Geneva, 1979
Represents Hanoi's official view: that the refugees were not fleeing persecution but spreading "anti-revolutionary propaganda." He argues that the United States is using the refugee crisis for Cold War political purposes.
— Full Panel Transcript · Washington D.C., 1979 —
Chen
Margaret Chen — Moderator Opening Good evening. It is 1979, four years after the Fall of Saigon. Over 130,000 Vietnamese have already been resettled in the United States Wikipedia, and thousands more arrive every month. President Carter has just doubled the monthly intake from 7,000 to 14,000 — despite 62% of Americans disapproving CBS/NYT Poll. Tonight we ask: Was taking in the Vietnamese refugees the right thing to do — and what does America owe people fleeing a war it helped start?

🖼 See Poster: The Numbers
Linh
Linh Thi Nguyen — Refugee Personal [speaking through a translator, voice steady but quiet] I was nine years old. We heard the helicopters before we saw them. My mother held my hand so tightly I thought my fingers would break. We ran toward the US Embassy — thousands of us were in the street. The Americans had promised to take their South Vietnamese allies. We had to climb a wall. A US Marine helped pull me over. I can still feel his hands.

We were on a ship for two days. Then a military plane. Then Fort Chaffee, Arkansas. I did not know the word "refugee." I only knew we were safe.

📖 See Comic Panel 1: The Evacuation
Taft
Julia Taft — U.S. Resettlement Director Defending Policy What Linh described is Operation Frequent Wind — the largest helicopter evacuation in history. In those final hours, U.S. aircraft made over 630 helicopter sorties and brought more than 1,500 South Vietnamese allies to safety Wikipedia / Op. Frequent Wind. But we knew that wasn't enough.

President Ford signed the Indochina Migration and Refugee Assistance Act on May 23, 1975 — just weeks after Saigon fell Wikipedia. Congress approved $455 million. We set up four military bases as processing centres: Camp Pendleton in California, Fort Chaffee in Arkansas, Eglin Air Force Base in Florida, and Fort Indiantown Gap in Pennsylvania IRC/Wikipedia. Thirty full-time IRC resettlement staff joined us on Day 1, along with hundreds of volunteers.

📖 See Comic Panel 2: The Camps 🖼 See Poster: Ford's Decision
Marsh
Rep. John Marsh — U.S. Congressman Opposition Nobody in this room wants to be called heartless. I am not heartless. But I represent a district in Ohio where unemployment is nearly nine percent CBS News 2025. My constituents are worried about their own jobs. And now we're being asked to spend $455 million on — as Senator Robert Byrd put it in the New York Times — a group that includes, quote, "undesirables" that haven't been properly screened Mercury News.

The Governor of California, Jerry Brown, said he did not want refugees settling in his state Mercury News. The Governor of Arkansas threatened to use the National Guard to keep them out of Fort Chaffee. I'm not saying these reactions are admirable. I am saying they are real — and they represent real Americans who feel their government is ignoring them.

📖 See Comic Panel 3: Public Opposition
Chen
Margaret Chen Follow-up Ms. Taft — the congressman raises a real tension. The unemployment rate in 1975 was near 9%, a post-World War II high CBS News. And a Gallup poll showed 54% of Americans opposed taking in Vietnamese refugees Wikipedia / Vietnamese Americans. President Ford ignored public opinion. Was that the right call for a democracy?
Taft
Julia Taft Moral Argument With respect to the Congressman — there was always public opposition to refugees. When Jewish refugees tried to flee Nazi Germany in 1939, a Gallup poll showed 67% of Americans opposed letting them in Boston Globe / IRC. We turned those ships away. We called it protecting American jobs. Hundreds of those people died in the Holocaust. That is a stain on this nation's history that we have never fully reckoned with.

President Ford knew that. He said — and I was in the room — "It just burns me up. We didn't do it to the Hungarians, we didn't do it to the Cubans, and damn it, we're not going to do it now." IRC / Boston Globe Leadership sometimes means doing the right thing before the polls catch up. That's what Ford did.

🖼 See Poster: Then and Now
Nguyễn V.L.
Nguyễn Văn Long — Vietnamese Government Counter-Argument Let us be clear about what is really happening here. The United States spent thirty years in our country. Your bombers dropped more explosives on Vietnam than all of World War II combined. You killed — by your own estimates — over three million Vietnamese History.com. Now you claim to be the rescuers?

These so-called "refugees" — many of them are members of the previous puppet regime, intelligence officers, people who collaborated with foreign military occupation. The so-called "re-education" was a necessary process of national reconciliation. Vietnam is rebuilding. If people fled, it is because foreign propaganda told them to fear their own government.

America does not take in these people out of kindness. It takes them in to embarrass Vietnam on the world stage. This is Cold War politics, not humanitarian aid.
Linh
Linh Thi Nguyen Direct Response [quietly, without translator] I learned this English sentence at Fort Chaffee. I practised it: "My father was a teacher."

Not a soldier. Not a spy. A teacher. He spent two years in a re-education camp — Camp Z30D — near the Cambodian border. When he came out, he shook all the time. He could not sleep. That is why we ran. Not because of propaganda. Because of what happened to him.

Over one million people were sent to those camps Wikipedia / Indochina Refugee Crisis. Were they all soldiers? Were they all collaborators? Or were some of them just teachers?

📖 See Comic Panel 4: Father's Story
— a long silence in the studio —
Chen
Margaret Chen Hard Question Congressman Marsh — you heard Linh. Her father was a schoolteacher. The refugees who were screened and processed at those four military bases — did any of them actually take American jobs? What does the evidence say?
Marsh
Rep. John Marsh Honest Reflection [long pause] In my district... no. Actually no. They mostly took jobs nobody else wanted. Some of them started small businesses that employed Americans. I'll admit — the refugees were dispersed across all 50 states intentionally, so no single area bore the full weight Wikipedia / Vietnamese Americans.

I still think we should have had a bigger public debate. I still think Congress acted too fast. But I will not look that girl in the eye and tell her father deserved to suffer. My objections are political. Her experience is human. And there is a difference.

📖 See Comic Panel 5: New Roots
Chen
Margaret Chen Present Day Question Ms. Taft — we're here because President Carter just doubled the monthly intake to 14,000 people, even with 62% public disapproval. He did it anyway. In your view, what lesson should the world take from how America has handled this crisis?
Taft
Julia Taft — Closing Statement Lesson The lesson is that fear and generosity can coexist — but you have to choose which one leads. By 1975, even some Members of Congress who had opposed the programme were quietly visiting the camps and coming away changed.

The Refugee Act of 1980 came directly from what we learned — it created the first permanent, systematic process for vetting and admitting refugees in American history IRC. Jimmy Carter signed it and called America "a country of refugees" IRC.

By 1992, more than one million Indochinese refugees had been admitted to the United States IRC. Today, Vietnamese-Americans have a median household income above the national average Quartz / Migration Policy. They are doctors, engineers, judges, Oscar-winning actors, restaurant owners who revived entire urban neighbourhoods. Boston's Fields Corner. Houston's Bellaire. Little Saigon in Orange County.

The refugees that 54% of Americans feared in 1975? They became, as they so often do, a blessing.

📖 See Comic Panel 6: The American Dream Arrived 🖼 See Poster: The Bet That Paid Off
Linh
Linh Thi Nguyen — Final Word Hopeful I am thirteen years old. I go to school in Arkansas. My teacher's name is Mrs. Patterson. She brought us cookies on the first day. She pointed at the American flag and said, "That flag is yours too now."

I did not know if I believed her. But I am starting to.

My father — still in Vietnam — cannot know I am safe. That is the part that hurts. But I will work hard enough for both of us. Because that is the deal — the deal America offered. And I intend to keep my side of it.

📚 Key Terms (Grade 10 Vocabulary)

Refugee
A person forced to flee their home country due to war, persecution, or natural disaster. Protected under international law (1951 UN Refugee Convention).
Parolee
A legal loophole used by the US to quickly admit Vietnamese refugees without going through the slow normal immigration process. It gave them temporary legal status.
Re-education Camps
Prisons run by the communist Vietnamese government after 1975. Over 1 million South Vietnamese — soldiers, officials, teachers — were sent there for "political re-education."
Fall of Saigon
April 30, 1975 — the day North Vietnamese Army tanks entered Saigon (now Ho Chi Minh City). It marked the end of the Vietnam War and triggered the refugee crisis.
Operation Frequent Wind
The US military's emergency helicopter evacuation from Saigon on April 29–30, 1975 — the largest helicopter evacuation in history, rescuing over 1,300 Americans and 5,500 Vietnamese.
Boat People
Vietnamese refugees who escaped by sea from 1978–1995. About 800,000 arrived safely; an estimated 200,000–250,000 died at sea from storms, piracy, and overcrowding.
Indochina Migration Act 1975
US law signed by President Ford on May 23, 1975. It approved $455 million for refugee resettlement and allowed 130,000 Southeast Asians to enter the United States.
Burden Sharing
The idea that multiple countries should share the responsibility of taking in refugees — rather than leaving it all to one country.

Why people fled

After 1975, the communist government ran "re-education camps" holding over 1 million people. Businesses were seized. Ethnic Chinese were persecuted. Hundreds of thousands were sent to "New Economic Zones" — forced labour in undeveloped land. Wikipedia

How the USA responded

Congress approved $455M within weeks. Four military bases became refugee camps. A team of NGOs — IRC, Church World Service, Lutheran Immigration — provided resettlement caseworkers. By 1992, 1 million+ resettled. IRC, Wikipedia

The outcome 50 years later

Vietnamese-Americans today number 2+ million. Median household income above national average. They include federal judges, Pulitzer Prize winners, Oscar winners ("My journey started on a boat" — Ke Huy Quan, 2023). CBS News 2025

Section 5 — Think Deeper

Discussion Questions & Key Takeaways

Use these questions for class discussion, written assignments, or personal reflection. They are designed for Grade 10 critical thinking — there is no single right answer.

💬 Discussion Questions

  • 1
    In 1975, 54% of Americans opposed taking in Vietnamese refugees. President Ford did it anyway. Do you think a democratic leader should follow public opinion or their own moral judgment? What are the risks of each approach? Give evidence from the panel.
  • 2
    Congressman Marsh worried about American jobs and welfare costs. Julia Taft argued America had a moral obligation. Whose argument is stronger? Can both be true at the same time? How would you balance compassion and practicality?
  • 3
    The Vietnamese diplomat Nguyễn Văn Long claims America was using the refugee crisis for Cold War politics, not genuine humanitarian reasons. Does a country need to have "pure" motives to do the right thing? Does it matter why America took in the refugees, as long as it did?
  • 4
    Linh says her father was "just a teacher" who was sent to a re-education camp. Nguyễn says these camps were necessary for "national reconciliation." What makes a policy fair or unfair? Who gets to decide?
  • 5
    Today there are 123 million displaced people in the world — the highest number ever recorded. What lessons from the Vietnamese refugee crisis (1975–1995) are most relevant today? What has the world learned — and what has it forgotten?
  • 6
    The comic strip shows Linh going from a refugee on a helicopter to a PhD holder decades later. Is this a success story — or does it put unfair pressure on refugees to "prove themselves"? Should refugees have to earn their welcome?

📋 Quick Reference: Key Laws & Events

April 30, 1975
Fall of Saigon. North Vietnamese Army enters Saigon. US embassy evacuated by helicopter. 130,000 Vietnamese flee to America.
May 23, 1975
President Ford signs the Indochina Migration and Refugee Assistance Act. $455 million approved. Four military bases set up as refugee camps.
1978–1979
Second wave of "boat people." 400,000 flee by sea. President Carter doubles monthly US intake to 14,000 despite 62% public opposition.
1979 Geneva Conference
International agreement: Southeast Asian countries give temporary shelter; rich countries take refugees permanently. The system that made mass resettlement possible.
Refugee Act of 1980
Signed by Carter. Created America's permanent, systematic refugee programme for the first time in history. Still in use today (in modified form).
1992
Over 1 million Indochinese refugees admitted to the US. The IRC alone helped resettle 120,000. Camps in Southeast Asia begin to close.
Today
2+ million Vietnamese-Americans. Median income above national average. Oscar winners, federal judges, restaurant owners who revived American neighbourhoods.
The Global Crisis Now
123.2 million displaced people worldwide (2024). 71% hosted by low-income countries. Resettlement numbers in 2025 are three times lower than 2024.
